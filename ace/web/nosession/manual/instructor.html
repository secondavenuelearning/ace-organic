ACE Organic Overview

Welcome to Ace Organic!

 

ACE Organic is an exciting new tool for students of introductory organic chemistry to learn, and to test their knowledge, and for their instructors to facilitate learning and monitor students' progress.

 

ACE Organic is a Web-based program developed specifically for instructors and students of introductory organic chemistry. In the typical organic chemistry course, students work questions out of a textbook for which a solutions manual is available. Students struggle with a question for a few minutes, look up the answer, and then feel as if they understand how to answer the question. It isn't until exam time that they realize that learning an answer to a question and knowing how to answer a question are two entirely different propositions.

 

Computer programs that can tell students that their responses are incorrect without giving away the correct answer are an ideal solution to this problem. Many Web-based chemistry homework programs exist, but all require text-based, numeric, or multiple-choice responses; none are well-suited to organic chemistry, where responses are usually structural drawings. ACE Organic permits students to construct structural responses to posed questions with a graphical structure-drawing interface. They submit their structure to a server, and, if their response is incorrect, they are given feedback and asked to try again.

 

What ACE Organic Allows Instructors to Do

Ace is a Web-based program that allows instructors to:

·         Set up an organic chemistry course or courses in ACE Organic.

·         Define and revise assignments.

·         Select questions that require building chemical structures from a database of questions.

·         Revise and create their own questions

·         Construct appropriate feedback to students.

·         Specify how many attempted responses are allowed on each question.

·         Communicate assignments and their due dates to students.

·         Decide whether and how to assign credit for a question.

·         Have grades assigned interactively, online.

·         View an individual's response to a question to better address any difficulty the student may be having.

·         View all students' responses to a question to understand how effectively material is being taught on that particular topic, or so the question can be clarified.

·         Track students' progress and grades on assignments.

 

What ACE Organic Allows Master Authors to Do

·         Everything Instructors can do

·         Add new topicss to the master database and populate them with new problem sets

·         Revise questions / responses on the master database

 

What ACE Organic Allows the Student to Do

·         Work assignments of organic chemistry problems interactively

·         Answer questions by building chemical structures with a graphical structure-drawing interface

·         Get immediate feedback on answers, including suggestions for how to correct and complete them

·         Revise each solution a fixed number of times (or an unlimited number at the instructor's option) until it is right.

·         Discuss problems with solutions with the instructor remotely, while the instructor views what the student has submitted.

·         Self-assess mastery of the material throughout the course, not just at exam time.

·         Review his/her grades on all the assignments in a course.

How ACE Organic Differs from Other Web-Based Homework Programs

Many Web-based interactive homework programs have been developed over the last ten years. What makes ACE Organic different?

  • ACE Organic requires students to draw structures in response to queries. The vast majority of Web-based homework programs require text-based, numerical, or multiple-choice responses. None of these response types is very suitable for organic chemistry.
  • Rather than telling a student what is the correct answer to a question, ACE Organic tells the student why a response is incorrect. ACE Organic evaluates various properties of a student's response and provides appropriate feedback. This feature corrects students' misperceptions and forces them to find the correct answer themselves, an exercise that is much more pedagogically useful to a student than simply being told the correct answer.
  • ACE Organic allows the instructor to modify or add to the question database. An instructor can add feedback for a student's mistake not already anticipated by ACE Organic's authors can fix an error in a database question. If the questions already in the database do not meet an instructor's needs, the instructor can write a whole series of new questions. At the same time, a large database of questions is already available, so if the instructor does not want to invest time in writing questions, he or she does not have to do so.
  • If a student is having trouble answering a question correctly, ACE Organic allows the instructor easily to see the last response the student has made. The instructor can then communicate suggestions to the student via email.
  • ACE Organic collects statistics on how students are performing on different questions. The instructor may use this information to improve his or her lectures.
  • ACE Organic has been designed so it can work with any textbook, although the content and organization of the ACE Organic question database has largely been drawn from the Prentice-Hall textbooks by Bruice and Wade.
  • The designers of ACE Organic are instructors themselves. As a result, ACE Organic is streamlined. It provides features that are necessary for teaching organic chemistry and omits features that are unnecessary.
  • The developers of ACE Organic are modifying it so it can handle mechanism questions. Students will be able to draw curved arrows to show electron flow, their answers will be evaluated, and feedback will be provided. The capability to handle conformation questions will also be added. And, yes, the capability to handle multistep-synthesis, text-based, and multiple-guess questions will eventually be added.

Components of ACE Organic

Students use two primary components of ACE Organic. The first is the student interface, which includes all the screens used to view profile, courses, assignments, and grades, as well as the screens through used to initiate working an assignment. The second is MarvinSketch (Java applet licensed from ChemAxon, Ltd.), which is used to draw chemical structures.

The behind-the-scenes components are JChem, also licensed from ChemAxon, Ltd., the program that has the "chemical intelligence" necessary to analyze structures, and Oracle, a database program.

These additional components available only to the instructor and/or Master Author:

·         The instructor tool, allows the instructor to assemble assignments from the question database and to review students' performance.

·         The authoring tool, allows an instructor or another qualified individual to create new questions, to modify existing questions, and to view the answers that students have given to a question.

·         A course-management program.

The ACE Organic administrator gives permission to instructors and authors to access the Instructor and/or Master Author capabilities. Instructors create courses within the course-management program and allow their students to access the content of those courses.

ACE Organic is already being used in classrooms across the USA. If you are an instructor and you would like to use ACE Organic, please contact us at ace-info@truth-n-beauty.com.

Questions, Answers, Feedback and Grading

 

Questions Currently Available

An ACE Organic question is one to which the response is a single chemical structure or a small group of structures. Questions regarding nomenclature, resonance structures, isomers, reactions, and spectroscopy are all handled well by ACE Organic. By contrast, questions on conformation, mechanism, and multistep-synthesis cannot be posed (yet). The plan is for future versions of ACE Organic will be able to handle questions of these types and others.

The current database contains about 400 questions that all instructors may use. An instructor uses a Web browser to assemble an assignment from questions in the database. The questions are generally organized by Paula Y. Bruice's plan in Organic Chemistry, but instructors are free to reorganize the questions into assignments in whatever way they want, so they are not required to use Bruice as their textbook. Instructors may also modify the existing questions or add their own new ones. Changes that one instructor makes to the question database are invisible to other instructors unless the instructor has been designated a "master author" by the ACE Organic administrator.

The future plan is to create a tool for incorporating instructors' additions and modifications into the master database (with their permission, of course). Prentice-Hall, the owner of ACE Organic, will decide questions of permission and remuneration.

 

Format of Questions and Answers

An ACE Organic question has a response whose format is a single chemical structure or a small group of structures. Questions may also cover nomenclature, resonance structures, isomers, reactions, and spectroscopy.

 

Options for Assigning Questions

Instructors have these options in setting up assignments:

1.      All students may see the same set of questions.

2.      Each student's questions may be randomly chosen from a larger group of questions.

Information Captured on Student Responses

ACE Organic records how many times a student has responded to a question, the student's last responses to that question, and the "quality" of that response (correct, partly correct, or incorrect, as determined by the question's author). Both student and instructor can see this information.

 

How Student Responses Are Judged

Each question is associated with a list of conditions which are met by correct, partly correct (the question author assigns a number between 0 and 1), and anticipated incorrect answers. The student's response is tested against each condition in turn. If the response meets a condition, the student receives feedback to that effect. If the response meets no conditions, the student is simply told that the response is incorrect. These conditions include:

  • If the answer is exactly …
  • If the answer is not exactly …
  • If the answer is exactly (without normalization) …
  • If the answer is not exactly (without normalization) …
  • If the answer is either enantiomer of …
  • If the answer is neither enantiomer of …
  • If the answer contains the substructure …
  • If the answer does not contain the substructure …
  • If the answer contains (among other molecules) …
  • If the answer does not contain (among other molecules) …
  • If the answer contains the skeleton …
  • If the answer does not contain the skeleton …
  • If the answer contains the sigma bond network …
  • If the answer does not contain the sigma bond network …
  • If the answer contains the functional group …
  • If the answer does not contain the functional group …
  • If the number of _ atoms is …
  • If the molecular weight is …
  • If the molecular weight is not …
  • If the exact mass is …
  • If the exact mass is not …
  • If the number of rings is …
  • If the charge is …
  • If the number of molecules in the answer is …
  • If the answer has the molecular formula …
  • If the answer doesn't have the molecular formula …

Rules for Evaluating Responses, including Stereochemical Issues

"If the answer is exactly …"

The response will meet this condition if the response contains all of the atoms and bonds present in the author's structure, and it contains no additional atoms or bonds.

Notes:

  • H atoms are ignored when structures are compared under this condition, whether they are present in the author's structure or the student's response. Charges and radicals, though, are not ignored.
  • Isotopically labelled atoms in the response or in the author's structure are considered to be different from unlabelled atoms.
  • A student's response or an author's structure may contain stereochemical information.
  • If the author's structure contains a tetrahedral stereocenter, and the author specifies its configuration as R, S, or a mixture of R and S (squiggly bond), then the stereocenter in the response must have the same configuration for an exact match to occur. If, on the other hand, the author does not specify the configuration of the stereocenter, the stereocenter in the response may have any configuration.
  • If the author's structure contains a stereogenic π bond, and the author draws its configuration as E or Z, then the π bond in the response must have the same configuration for an exact match to occur. If, on the other hand, the author specifies the configuration of the π bond as a mixture of E and Z (criss-cross bond) or has drawn a 180° bond angle to one of the alkene substituents, the π bond in the response may have any configuration.
  • MarvinSketch has no convenient way to designate relative (as opposed to absolute) stereochemistry. If the author wants to test for relative stereochemistry only, the "either enantiomer" condition should be used.

"If the answer is exactly (without normalization) …"

Ylides and aromatics can be drawn in more than resonance form. ACE Organic normalizes ylides and aromatics to a single conventional resonance form before comparing them. As a result, the structures Me2S+-O- and Me2S="O" are viewed as identical compounds, as are the two aromatic resonance forms of 1,2-dichlorobenzene.

The normalization process occurs by default, but there are some types of questions, chiefly those of the "draw a resonance structure" type, in which normalization is not desired. The "exactly (without normalization)" condition allows ACE Organic to distinguish different resonance structures of ylides and aromatics. If different resonance structures should not be distinguished, use the regular "exactly" condition.

"If the answer is either enantiomer of …"

The response will meet this condition if the response is exactly the author's structure or its mirror image. The mirror image is generated mathematically, so a stereocenter with indeterminate configuration will also have indeterminate configuration in the mirror image.

"If the answer contains (among other molecules) …"

Use this condition when the response may contain multiple molecules and you wish to give specific feedback if any one of them is present or absent. The rules of the "exactly" condition are used to check each molecule in the response against the author's structure. An author should draw only a single structure in association with this condition. If you want to check the presence or absence of more than one molecule in the response, use a complex condition.

"If the answer contains the substructure …"

The response will meet this condition if:

  • all of the non-H atoms of the substructure are present in the response;
  • all of the explicit H atoms of the substructure are present (either implicit or explicit) in the response (implicit H atoms in the substructure are ignored);
  • all of the bonds of the substructure are present in the response, and they are the same order (single, double, or triple).

Notes:

  • Implicit H atoms in substructures (the ones automatically included by MarvinSketch but not explicitly drawn out by the author) are ignored. For example, if you want to check the presence of CH3CH2 in the answer, you must draw out the five hydrogen atoms and their bonds to the two carbons. If you omit them, any response with a C-C chain (not a CH3-CH3 group, even though that is what MarvinSketch shows) will contain the substructure.
  • The bond order in the response must exactly match the bond orders in the substructure for the response to meet the condition. For example, CH2="CH"2 does not contain the C-C substructure.
  • Do not use this condition for identifying functional groups. Separate conditions for identifying the presence or absence of functional groups are available.
  • Stereochemistry is not ignored in this condition. If a particular configuration of a stereocenter is indicated in a substructure, the corresponding stereocenter in the response must have exactly that configuration for the response to contain the substructure.

"If the answer has the skeleton …"

The response will meet this condition if:

  • all of the non-H atoms of the skeleton are present in the response;
  • all of the explicit H atoms of the skeleton are present (either implicit or explicit) in the response (implicit H atoms in the skeleton are ignored);
  • all of the bonds of the skeleton are present in the response, but in the response they may have the same or a higher order;
  • the response contains no nonskeletal C atoms attached to skeletal C atoms (but nonskeletal C atoms may be attached to skeletal heteroatoms); and,
  • all bonds between two skeletal C atoms in the response are also present in the skeleton (but bonds between skeletal C atoms and skeletal or nonskeletal heteroatoms in the response may be absent in the skeleton).

Examples:

  • Anisole has the benzene skeleton.
  • Toluene does not have the benzene skeleton.
  • Anisole has the phenol skeleton.
  • Benzene does not have the phenol skeleton.
  • Cyclohexene has the cyclohexane skeleton.
  • Bicyclo[3.1.0]hexane does not have the cyclohexane skeleton.
  • Cyclohexene oxide has the cyclohexanol skeleton.
  • Cyclohexane does not have the cyclohexene skeleton.

Stereochemistry is ignored when a structure is analyzed for a skeleton. Only connectivity matters.

"If the answer has the same sigma-bond network as …"

The response will meet this condition if the number and connections of atoms and σ bonds in the response, including implicit H atoms, exactly matches the number and connections of atoms and σ bonds in the author's structure, including implicit H atoms. The two structures may differ in the number and location of π bonds, charges, and unshared electrons.

Stereochemistry is not ignored in this condition. If a particular configuration of a stereocenter is indicated in a sigma-bond network, the corresponding stereocenter in the response must have exactly that configuration for the response to have that sigma-bond network.

This condition is designed to test for resonance structures.

"If the answer has the functional group …"

Use the functional-group finder to see a list of the functional groups known by ACE Organic. Charges and radicals are ignored when a molecule is searched for a functional group.

"If the answer has the molecular formula …"

This condition is self-explanatory, but a few notes are in order.

  • Hill order (C and H first, others alphabetical) is preferred for ease of reading, but it is not mandatory.
  • If an element that is not specified in the formula is present in the response, then the response does not have that formula.
  • Use * (e.g., C6H10O*S) to allow an indeterminate number (0 or more) of any atom in a structure. To require one or more of any atom, combine the conditions, e.g., "If the answer has the molecular formula C6H10O*S …" and, "If the number of O atoms is more than 1 …" with the OR operator.

A Typical Question and Its Conditions

Question: Draw the major product of the following reaction:

Correct answers

Feedback

1

If the answer is exactly E-2-butene

Good!

2

(Partially correct) If the answer is exactly Z-2-butene

Close, but the major product is the one lowest in energy.

 

Wrong answers

Feedback

1

If the answer is exactly 1-butene

The answer is indeed an alkene, but remember Zaitsev's rule about regiochemistry.

2

If the answer is exactly methanol

Yes, this is one product of the reaction, but I'm looking for the other product.

3

If the number of K atoms is more than 0

Potassium (K) is not incorporated into the organic product.

4

If the number of Br atoms is more than 0

The Br atom is a leaving group in this reaction. It is not contained in the product.

5

(5.1) or (5.2)
   5.1 If the answer contains the functional group ether
   5.2 If the answer contains the substructure methoxy

The secondary alkyl halide is more likely to undergo elimination than substitution when it reacts with this strong, small base.

6

If the number of C atoms is not equal to 4

No C-C bonds are expected to be made or broken in this reaction. Count your carbons, please.

  1. Register with ACE Organic by going to the ACE Organic Login page, and press Register.
  2. From the dropdown menu, choose Student. The Student Registration page will appear.
  3. Enter the requested information.
  • Define a username that you will use to log into the ACE Organic system.

  7. The links at the top of the My Courses page, Course Info page, and many other pages may be clicked at any time.

o        Logout logs you out of ACE Organic.

How to Work an Assignment

  1. Login to ACE Organic. You will be brought to the My Courses page. (If you forget your user ID or password, contact the ACE Organic administrator. Do not reregister with the system.)

  3. Click the Assignments tab. The Assignments / Do Assignment page will open.
  4. Click on the link for the appropriate assignment.

The Problem List page will appear. The assignment name, the maximum number of times you are allowed to try each question, and your instructor's remarks (if any) are given at the top of the page. The assignment questions occupy the remainder of the page. A symbol (or the lack of one) next the each question's number shows its status:

  • Correctly answered questions have a green checkmark.
  • Questions that have been attempted but not answered correctly have a red “X” next to the problem .
  • If no symbol appears, the question has not yet been attempted.

  1. Press Submit Answer and wait for the response.

  1. When you are finished, press any of the buttons or tabs at the top of the page to move to another ACE Organic screen, or Logout.

Designing Response Conditions and Feedback

The usefulness of ACE Organic is directly tied to the usefulness of the feedback provided to students who have entered incorrect answers. Here are some guidelines for designing conditions that test for incorrect answers.

General Advice

  1. The order of conditions is important. A student's response is tested against conditions in the order in which the conditions are listed. For example, if condition 2 is, "If the answer is not a ketone," it follows that any response that is tested against subsequent conditions must be a ketone.
  2. It is better to put conditions that rule out wildly inaccurate answers earlier in the sequence. For example, if a question asks for the product of an SN2 reaction, conditions that test for responses that retain the leaving group are better placed early in the list of conditions. Once outrageous answers are ruled out, only a limited number of answers is left to consider.
  3. Make liberal use of conditions involving functional groups, numbers of atoms, substructures, and skeletons. Be conservative in your use of conditions that test responses against specific structures. If you enter a separate condition for every wrong answer a student might enter, rather than enter conditions that test for classes of wrong answers, either your conditions will fail to catch many wrong answers, or your questions will quickly become unwieldy.
  4. Review the rules for matching molecules, substructures, and skeletons to understand how stereochemical issues are handled. This also explains when it is important to draw out explicitly the C-H bonds in substructures.
  5. Design your feedback so that it guides the student to the correct answer without giving it away. For example, if a student enters an anti-Markovnikov addition product, remind her of Markovnikov's rule without telling her that the correct answer has the nucleophile on the more substituted C atom.
  6. Include feedback even for correct answers. Sometimes a student will guess a correct answer without knowing why the answer is correct. A statement like, "Right! The Markovnikov product, in which the nucleophile adds to the more substituted C, is obtained predominantly," is much more useful than, "Right!"
  7. When you enter or modify a question, always use the Preview button to test how well your conditions catch wrong answers both reasonable and outrageous.
  8. During the course, use the View responses button to mold conditions and feedback to the errors your students make. The question database should be regarded as evolving, not static.

Specific Advice

  1. If a solvent is indicated, but it does not participate in the reaction, then test for elements in the solvent that are not present in either reagent. For example, if the question is Me2S + CH3I in EtOH, one of the conditions would be, "If the number of O atoms is more than 0…", and the feedback would be, "The solvent does not participate in the reaction."
  2. In functional group interchange reactions, test for the absence of the skeleton of the product. If the condition is fulfilled, the feedback is, "No C-C bonds are likely to be made or cleaved in this reaction. Count your carbons." The double feedback deals with both intentional and unintentional skeletal changes.
  3. Although ACE Organic does not have an "Else" condition, you can invent one by entering a condition that is opposite to a previous one. For example, if one condition is, "If the number of Br atoms is equal to one," an "Else" condition could be, "If the number of Br atoms is not equal to one…"

Formatting Text in ACE Organic

EPOCH allows an author to enter rich text (subscripts, superscripts, bold, etc.) and symbols in the question statement, feedback, reaction data or molecule name. An author familiar with HTML tags could enter them directly in the textboxes to manipulate the appearances of text in the browser (see note below). However, EPOCH provides a set of easy-to-use notations for specifying common chemical formatting and symbols, and we recommend that you use them whenever possible. No knowledge of HTML is needed.

Subscripting

Numbers following elemental symbols and ) characters are subscripted automatically. For example, if you enter H2SO4 in a text box, it will be displayed as H2SO4, and Ca(SO4)2 will be rendered as Ca(SO4)2. If you want to override this rule, use the ^ (shift-6) symbol just before the number. For example, C^4 will be rendered as C4.

Both SN1 and SN2 are automatically rendered as SN1 and SN2.

Superscripting

Enter ^ before a character to superscript that character and no subsequent ones, and enter ^^ to superscript all subsequent characters until a space character. For example, OH^- will be rendered as OH-, and cm^^-1 will be rendered as cm-1 . Only numbers and the + and - signs can be superscripted.

Both sp2 and sp3 are automatically rendered as sp2 and sp3.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) about ACE Organic (for Students)

Q: ACE Organic doesn't seem to work. When I press a button, nothing happens.
A: Read the Technical Requirements here.

Q: When I log in, no courses are listed.
A: Either your instructor has not uploaded the enrollment list yet, or the student ID that you entered doesn't match the one on the enrollment list. Click My profile, and make sure that you typed your ID correctly and that you correctly entered (or omitted) spaces and hyphens.

Q: How many times can I try to answer a question?
A: The number is set by your instructor when the assignment is constructed. It is visible at the upper right of the question-answering window.

Q: I never get any feedback for my wrong answers.
A: Feedback is generated when you submit a wrong answer that the author of the question anticipated. If you are generating many unanticipated answers, it may be that your answers are very far off the mark. A review of the material might help. Also be sure that you aren't drawing structures with atoms underlined in red. The red underline indicates an atom that has an incorrect valence.

Q: I can't seem to get MarvinSketch to do what I want.
A: Try the MarvinSketch tutorial. If it's not available, ask your instructor to make it available.

Q: Are there more questions I can try?
A: Your instructor can write more questions and construct more assignments from existing questions.

Q: Are there any mechanism or multistep-synthesis questions for me to try?
A: Not at this time, although they may be added in the future. Meanwhile, resonance, nomenclature, stereochemistry, spectroscopy, and draw-the-product questions can be handled by ACE Organic. All of them require a single organic structure as an answer.
